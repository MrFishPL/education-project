LIFE

Karol Wojtyla was born on 18th of May 1920 in Wadowice. His family lived modestly, living on the salary of his father, who worked as a military officer in the District Enlargement Commission. Little Karol loved football, skiing and sightseeing tours. He graduated from primary school in 1931 and attended the State Men’s Gymnasium in Wadowice. He learned very well and was characterized by a strong faith, which was reflected, among other things, in the performance of his duties as an acolyte in church. In May 1938, he passed the matura exam with very good results. In October he studied Polish at the Faculty of Philosophy of the Jagiellonian University and moved to Cracow with his father. His father died in February 1941 after a long illness. As a result of the war, Wojtyla was unable to continue his studies and worked in the Solvay chemical factory in 1940. From autumn 1940 he worked for a year in the quarry in Zakrzówek and later in the water treatment plant in Borek Falecki. From 1941 to 1942 he collaborated with the Rhapsodisches Theater. In 1942 he decided to study theology and entered the secret Metropolitan Seminary in Krakow. He was ordained a priest in 1946. The following years of his life were a period of intense theological work, collaboration with Catholic magazines and ascension in the ecclesiastical hierarchy. He was appointed bishop in 1958, archbishop in 1963, and cardinal in 1967. After the death of Pope John Paul I in 1978, he was elected Pope at the Second Conclave and took the name John Paul II. His pontificate, which lasted more than 26 years, was one of the longest in history. John Paul II had a great influence on the changes in Europe, including the collapse of communism. In 1981 he miraculously survived an assassination attempt by the bullet of the professional assassin Ali Agcy, to whom he showed forgiveness during a visit to the prison. He was known as a great devotee of ecumenism and a pilgrim. On his 104th pilgrimages, he visited 134 countries, including eight times his homeland. He was a highly esteemed moral authority. He died on 2 April 2005 in the Vatican. He was appointed Venerable Servant of God by Pope Benedict XVI on 19 December 2009 and beatified on 1 May 2011. His canonization took place on 27 April 2014 in the presence of the two Roman Bishops, the incumbent Pope Francis and the emeritus Benedict XVI.

PONTIFICATE

On 16 October 1978, Cardinal Karol Wojtyła was elected Pope. This election interrupted the tradition of the name that Italian clergymen had been called to the throne of Peter for centuries. At 6:18 p.m., a white smoke rose above the Sistine Chapel, which caused cheers among those gathered in St Peter’s Square. The inauguration of the pontificate took place on 22 October. During the sermon, John Paul II spoke the famous words: “Do not be afraid! Open the door to Christ.” The pontificate of Poland was the third longest in history, lasting 26 years and 5 months. John Paul II introduced the Church into the new millennium, travelled around the world with the message of peace, contributed to the overthrow of the communist system, defended the rights of the family and unborn children, cared for the sick, the poor and the elderly, met with followers of different faiths and religions, established World Youth Day, proclaimed the new Code of Canon Law and the Catechism of the Catholic Church. John Paul II undertook 104 Apostolic Journeys abroad (outside Italy), during which he visited 129 nations and planned a total of 3288 addresses. He made 146 trips throughout Italy and 748 visits to Rome and the diocese of Rome and Castel Gandolfo. The Pope met 1,600 times for official and informal meetings with heads of state and prime ministers and held 1,164 general audiences attended by more than 17 million people from all over the world. John Paul II published 14 Encyclicals, 11 Apostolic Constitutions, 14 Apostolic Letters, 45 Apostolic Letters and 31 Motu Proprio. He beatified 1338 people and made 482 canonizations. Pope John Paul II initiated the World Youth Days, the World Days of Prayer for Peace, the Jubilee Years and the World Meetings of Families. John Paul II.He went to Poland eight times. He arrived for the first time on June 2, 1979, and this pilgrimage coincided with the celebration of the 900th anniversary of the martyrdom of St Stanislaus of Stephen. At that time he spoke in Victory Square in Warsaw (now Pilsudskiego Square) the memorable words: “May your spirit come down! And He will renew the face of the earth.” Other pilgrimages took place in 1983 and 1987 during the period of the People’s Republic of Poland and contributed to the founding of Solidarność and the deconstruction of the system of that time. In 1991 he made his first pilgrimage to free Poland, on which he exhorted the Poles to make good use of their regained freedom. Further visits took place in the Altas in 1995, 1997 and 1999. His last pilgrimage in 2002, at the Shrine of Krakow – Łagiewniki, he entrusted the whole world to the mercy of God.

DEATH

On May 13, 1981, the anniversary of the apparitions of Fatima, John Paul II experienced an attack on his life. Mehmet Ali Agca shot the Pope from a distance of three and a half metres. He attributed his salvation to the intercession of Our Lady. He said, “One hand shot, the other directed the bullet.” He met with his assassins in prison and showed him forgiveness. In the late 1990s, the Pope was diagnosed with Parkinson’s disease, which worsened over time. Nevertheless, John Paul II travelled and was fully in his service to the end of his days. Before his death, he lost his voice and was therefore unable to speak to the faithful. He went to his father’s house at 9:37 p.m. on April 2, 2005. At his funeral on April 8, the Gospel on the coffin was enveloped by the wind, which could be interpreted as a sign that his life led by the Holy Spirit was completely fulfilled. The funeral celebrations in St Peter’s Square were attended by 300,000 faithful, and two billion watched the broadcast on television. The coffin with the body was placed in the cellar of St. Peter’s Cathedral.
