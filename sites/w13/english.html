General information

Irena Stanisława Sendler, née Krzyżanowska, nom de guerre “Jolanta”, was born on 15 February 1910 in Warsaw, and died on 12 May 2008 also in Warsaw. She was a Polish social activist and humanitarian, and head of the children’s section of “Żegota”, the Polish Council to Aid Jews, during World War II. She received many important decorations, such as: Righteous Among the Nations or Order of the White Eagle. She was involved in helping the Jews from the beginning of the German occupation. In 1943 she was arrested by the Gestapo, however “Żegota” managed to free her. She was a nurse during the Warsaw Uprising, and after the war, she worked in social care, state administration, and secondary medical education. She was nominated for the Nobel Peace Prize in 2007 and 2008.

Biography

Youth

Sendler’s mother was Janina Krzyżanowska, and her father was Stanisław Krzyżanowski, who was a doctor. As a child, Irena lived in Warsaw, however due to her illness, her family moved to Otwock. The girl had no siblings, so she would willingly play with her Jewish peers, from whom she learned the Yiddish language. In 1917 Sendler’s father died from epidemic typhus, and she moved with her mother to Tarczyn and later on to Piotrkowo Trybunalskie. That’s where she met her first future husband Mieczysław. In 1927, she left to study in the Faculty of Law at the University of Warsaw. After 2 years she transferred to the Faculty of Humanities. However, she stopped her studies in 1932-1937. She worked i.a. in the Mother and Child Care Section, the Legal Clinic of the Committee or in the Care and Health Center. In 1931 she got married to Mieczysław Sendler.

World war II

During the German occupation, she was employed as a social worker. She had completed a nursing course and worked in health centers. In 1939, helping the Jewish population was deemed as forbidden. But despite that, a group of aid workers organized by Sendler still continued to help Jews. She had obtained ID cards for employees of the sanitary column who fought against infectious diseases in the ghetto for herself and Irena Schultz. The pass allowed entering the ghettos, so Sendler and Schultz made use of it and kept bringing food, clothes, money and medicines to the ghettos. She had been preparing fake community interviews, changing Jewish children's resumes. This allowed them to be referred to care facilities as Polish orphans. Sendler also helped adults escape.

In 1942, she made contact with the Council to Aid Jews “Żegota” and reported to the Welfare and Health Department operating in the underground. She was subordinated to the management of Żegota. Sendler looked after 8-10 flats in which Jews were hiding. In 1943, she became the head of the "Żegota’s" children's section and led an encrypted file of children and adults covered by the aid. In 1943, she was arrested by the Gestapo because she was exposed by the owner of the laundry. “Żegota” managed to save her by paying a large bribe. After being freed, she continued her activities under the name of Klara Dąbrowska. She became a nurse during the uprising in Mokotów and participated in the establishment of the hospital. According to post-war reports, as a result of the actions of Żegota, an attempt to save about 2,500 Jewish children had been made, but not all of them managed to survive the war.

Post-war period

In 1945 Sendler was offered a job in the Social Welfare Department, so she started organizing orphanages, old people's homes and nursing homes. After the merger of the Polish Workers’ Party and the Polish Socialist Party, she became a member of the Polish United Workers' Party. After that, she had worked in many places, but she eventually retired in 1967. In 1965 she was awarded the title of Righteous Among the Nations, and in 1991 she was granted honorary citizenship of Israel. After the war, she was questioned many times by functionaries of the Public Security Office, because she had been accused of employing members of the Home Army. In 1980, she joined the Independent Self-governing Trade Union "Solidarity".

After the war, she began to experience health problems caused by constant stress and head injuries sustained during Gestapo interrogations. She spent the last years of her life at the Geriatric and Rehabilitation Home of the Convent of Brothers in Warsaw. She died on May 12 2008, and the funeral took place on May 15 at the Powązki Cemetery in Warsaw.

In 2013, mementos of Irena Sendler, such as: the medal and diploma of the Righteous Among the Nations, honorary citizenship of Israel, honorary citizenship of the city of Otwock along with the key to the city gates were donated to the Museum of the History of Polish Jews in Warsaw by her daughter Janina.

  • Gold Cross of Merit (1947)

Private life

In 1931, she married Mieczysław Sendler, a junior assistant at the Faculty of Classical Philology at the University of Warsaw. However, they divorced in 1947. She married Stefan Zgrzembski the same year. He was a Jew whom she met during her university years. They had three children: Janina, Andrzej and Adam. However, the marriage did not work out yet again and the couple divorced in 1961. After Zgrzembski's death, she got married to Mieczysław Sendler again, who had remarried and widowed after their separation. After 10 years of marriage, they decided to part ways, but they still remained friends for the rest of their lives.

Irena Sendler Award
“For Repairing The World”

In 2006, the Association of the Children of the Holocaust together with the Ministry of Foreign Affairs established the Irena Sendler Award under the name “For Repairing The World”. It is awarded to teachers and tutors. Every year, by the decision of the jury in the United States and Poland, a prize of 10,000 dollars is awarded to two winners from both of these countries.
